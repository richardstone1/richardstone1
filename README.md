## Engineering Philosophy
I rarely begin with a complete answer. My process is iterative: follow a plausible path, test it against reality, measure what happens, find what I missed, refine the question, and repeat. 

After four decades, I’ve found that the best engineering isn't about knowing the answer—it's about knowing how to ask the right questions. I treat every project as an opportunity to pressure-test my own intuition against the reality of the hardware. 

The more experience I've gained, the more I value reviewing my own assumptions before declaring a problem solved. Engineering progress often comes from discovering that the original question was incomplete. The other half of that discipline is knowing when the evidence is sufficient, the design is sound, and it is time to finalize.

> Measurements beat assumptions. Data beats speculation. Documented results beat anecdotes.
